# CBSE Robotics & Coding Curriculum 2025

The National Education Policy (NEP) 2020 fundamentally changed Indian school education: it made **coding and computational thinking mandatory from Class 6 onwards**. Five years on, the rollout is uneven — and that creates both a problem and an opportunity for students and parents.

## What NEP 2020 actually mandates

The NEP 2020 framework calls for:

- **Coding from Class 6** — block-based programming foundations
- **Integration with science and math** — coding as a way to *do* science, not a separate subject
- **AI, ML and robotics at higher classes** — Class 9-12 specialisation paths
- **Skill-based assessment** alongside theoretical exams
- **21st-century skills**: computational thinking, design thinking, problem-solving

CBSE has rolled this out via a combination of mandatory and elective subjects. The actual on-the-ground experience varies dramatically school to school.

## Grade-wise breakdown

**Class 6** — introduction to block-based coding (Scratch, MIT App Inventor concepts), basic logic and algorithms. Hands-on activities like sequencing, loops, conditionals.

**Class 7-8** — extension of block coding, introduction to Arduino-style microcontroller basics in better-equipped schools. Concepts of sensors, actuators, simple robotics.

**Class 9-10** — CBSE elective subject **Artificial Intelligence (Code 417)** is widely adopted. Covers AI fundamentals, ethics, project-based learning. Robotics is part of this in many schools.

**Class 11-12** — CBSE offers **Robotics (Subject Code 770)** as a vocational subject. Includes Arduino programming, sensors, actuators, AI basics, real project work.

## The reality check

Most CBSE schools have one of three setups:

1. **Best case** — an [Atal Tinkering Lab](/blog/atal-tinkering-lab-guide-india), trained teachers, active robotics club, students winning state-level competitions. About 10% of CBSE schools.
2. **Common case** — robotics taught from textbook with minimal hardware. Students "learn" Arduino without ever touching one. About 60%.
3. **Lagging case** — robotics paper exists on the timetable but isn't actually taught. About 30%.

If you're a parent: ask the school which of these three you're in. The honest answer matters.

## How to get ahead of the curriculum

The most effective Class 6-10 students don't wait for school to catch up. They:

- Spend ₹500 on an Arduino kit and start at home (see [our kits guide](/blog/best-robotics-kits-india-2025))
- Use free resources like R2BOT Atlas to read about concepts before they're "taught"
- Build at least one competition-ready project per year — line follower, obstacle avoider, robot arm
- Join WRO India, Robofest, or e-Yantra Junior competitions

By Class 11, a self-directed student who started in Class 6 is 4 years ahead of the CBSE curriculum. That's how you stand out in college admissions.

## CBSE's official robotics resources

- **NCERT digital initiatives** — DIKSHA portal has approved content for coding and AI subjects.
- **CBSE skill curriculum books** — paperback editions for the AI 417 and Robotics 770 subjects. Often available as free PDFs from CBSE's website.
- **Atal Tinkering Lab equipment** — already in 10,000+ schools nationwide. Many CBSE schools have one but don't use it actively.

## What parents should actually push for

Three things make the biggest difference:

1. **An active ATL** — if the school has one, demand it's used. Run a parent-teacher meeting on this if needed.
2. **A real subject teacher** — not the IT teacher teaching robotics by reading from a textbook. Many schools have access to NASSCOM-trained or CBSE-certified robotics educators.
3. **Competition participation** — schools that send teams to WRO, ATL Marathon or Robofest take robotics seriously. Schools that don't, often don't.

## Which private school chains are doing this best

Without endorsement of any chain in particular, the schools consistently producing competition-level robotics students are: DPS chains in metros, Indus International, Pathways World School, Singapore International School (Mumbai), and select Kendriya Vidyalayas with active ATLs.

The pattern isn't about prestige or fees. It's about having one teacher who genuinely cares and a school administration that funds equipment and competition trips.

## The career angle for parents

Robotics is one of NASSCOM's projected top-five hiring areas through 2030. The ₹6-25 LPA salary ranges discussed in our [career guide](/blog/robotics-career-india-salary-jobs-2025) are real. But the path requires depth — not surface-level exposure.

NEP 2020 created the framework. What you do with it depends on you.
