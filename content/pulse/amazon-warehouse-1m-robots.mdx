Amazon now operates more than one million robots across its global fulfilment network. According to a press release this week, that number passed the number of humans Amazon employs in those same warehouses for the first time, hitting roughly 1.05 million robots against 1.0 million human warehouse associates.

The headline is impressive. What's underneath is the more interesting story: most of those robots don't look like robots at all.

## The five Amazon robots, none of which are humanoid

Amazon's warehouse robotics is dominated by five distinct machine types. None of them walks. None of them has arms or a face.

**Hercules** — Amazon's flagship mobile drive unit. A flat, orange, knee-high box that scoots under a storage shelf, lifts the entire shelf, and carries it across the warehouse to a human picking station. There are roughly 750,000 of these worldwide. Modern versions navigate using a mix of [SLAM](/atlas/concept/slam), QR code markers on the floor, and centralised traffic control.

**Pegasus** — Hercules's smaller sibling, with a conveyor belt on top. Pegasus carries individual packages between processing stations rather than whole shelves. About 200,000 in service.

**Robin and Sparrow** — Two robotic arms. Robin sorts packages onto Pegasus drives at high speed. Sparrow picks individual items out of bins (the hardest manipulation problem in the warehouse — every item is a different size, weight, and texture). The latest Sparrow can handle about 65% of Amazon's product catalogue, up from 14% in 2022.

**Proteus** — Amazon's first "fully autonomous mobile robot" — meaning, the first one that can operate in the same physical space as humans without safety cages. Slow, careful, lots of sensors. About 50,000 Proteus units now run alongside humans.

**Digit** — The lone humanoid. Built by Agility Robotics, not Amazon. Amazon has been piloting Digit in a few facilities since 2023 for the specific job of moving plastic totes from one shelf to another. The pilot is going slowly. Humanoids are still hard.

## Why this matters for the humanoid debate

A lot of the humanoid hype — Tesla [Optimus](/atlas/robot/optimus), Figure 03, the Unitree H1 — is justified by pointing at Amazon-style warehouse work and saying, "Replace these humans with humanoids."

Amazon's data tells a different story: in the highest-volume warehouse on earth, humanoids are about 0.005% of the robot fleet. The other 99.995% are specialised, not general-purpose. They're cheaper. They're more reliable. They're optimised for one job.

This doesn't mean humanoids won't matter — they'll be essential the day a robot needs to climb stairs or open a fridge. But it does mean the case for general-purpose humanoids has to be earned, not assumed. The current evidence is that for known, repetitive tasks in known environments, *specialised beats general* by a wide margin.

## What's next at Amazon

Amazon has hinted at a few things in its press materials:

- A new generation of Sparrow arms targeting 90% catalogue coverage by 2027.
- A robotic packaging system that builds custom-sized boxes around individual items, eliminating wasted shipping volume.
- More Proteus rollout — the safety case for human-robot shared spaces is now proven.

Notably absent: any announcement about humanoids at scale.

## What it means for everyone

This is the first time in industrial history that an employer of a million-plus humans operates more robots than humans in the same role. The transition has been gradual — Amazon went from 0 robots in 2012 (when it acquired Kiva Systems for $775 million) to a million in 2026. Fourteen years. That's roughly the time it takes a human to grow from a baby to a teenager.

The 2025-2035 decade is when most heavy-industrial workplaces will cross the same threshold. Robotics is no longer a thing that's coming. It's a thing that's here.
