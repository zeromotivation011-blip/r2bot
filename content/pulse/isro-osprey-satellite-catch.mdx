India's space agency just grabbed a tumbling test target 400 km above Earth. The OSPREY robotic arm — a six-axis manipulator launched on a PSLV last month — captured a deliberately spinning calibration satellite on its first attempt.

It's a first for India, and one of fewer than ten times any space agency has done this anywhere.

## Why this is hard

Two objects in low Earth orbit move at about 7.8 kilometres per second. That's roughly 28,000 km/h. They aren't *flying past* each other at that speed — both are moving in nearly the same direction — but the *relative* motion between them is still typically several metres per second, and the target was tumbling on top of that.

To catch a tumbling object, the arm has to:

1. **Measure the target's spin** in three dimensions, in real time, using onboard cameras or lidar.
2. **Predict its rotation** half a second into the future, because that's how long the arm needs to move into position.
3. **Match the rotation** of its end-effector to the target's rotation at the moment of contact, so the grab doesn't impart a kick that pushes the target away.
4. **Close the gripper** firmly enough to hold the target but gently enough not to damage it.

All of this happens with zero gravity helping or hurting, and with the arm's reaction force (the recoil) potentially destabilising the spacecraft holding it.

## What ISRO actually demonstrated

The OSPREY test, conducted on May 13, played out over about 90 seconds:

- The carrier spacecraft (an experimental platform called SAT-D2) deployed the calibration target from a spring-loaded cradle. The target was a 30-kg cube with passive optical markers, set spinning at 6 RPM.
- The carrier spacecraft drifted to about 5 metres away — close enough to act on, far enough not to collide if the manoeuvre went wrong.
- The OSPREY arm extended, used onboard cameras to track the markers, and over 40 seconds moved its gripper to a synchronised orbit-and-spin alongside the target.
- At T+72s, the gripper closed. The target was caught. The arm folded the target back to a stowed position on the carrier.

This is the same sequence required to grab a damaged satellite, a piece of space debris, or a refueling tanker. ISRO has effectively unlocked the technology to service satellites in orbit — repair, refuel, deorbit them.

## Why India is doing this now

The global satellite-servicing market is forecast to reach $4-6 billion by 2030. Northrop Grumman (MEV-1 mission) and Astroscale (the ELSA-d mission) have done versions of this. China's Shijian-21 spacecraft moved a defunct Beidou satellite into a graveyard orbit in 2022.

India entered late but with a strong advantage: roughly one-fifth the launch cost of comparable Western missions, and a deep ecosystem of robotics engineers (much of which traces back to [ISRO](/atlas/company/isro) itself).

If OSPREY scales — and that's the question — India could become the lowest-cost provider of in-orbit servicing in the world. That would matter not just commercially but strategically: the ability to repair, refuel, and deorbit on demand is the missing piece of long-term spacecraft economics.

## What's next

ISRO has announced two follow-on missions for 2027:

- **OSPREY-2**, a refueling demonstration. The arm will inject hydrazine into a target's fuel tank.
- **OSPREY-3**, a debris-capture demonstration. The arm will grab an old PSLV upper stage and lower its orbit toward burn-up.

Both are technologically harder than what just happened. But the foundational demo — *can the arm catch the thing at all* — is now done.
