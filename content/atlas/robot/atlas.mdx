Atlas is [Boston Dynamics](/atlas/company/boston-dynamics)' bipedal [humanoid robot](/atlas/concept/humanoid-robot) — for over a decade the most acrobatic [robot](/atlas/concept/robot) ever built. It has done backflips, parkour, dance routines, and obstacle courses while every other walking robot was still struggling to climb a flight of stairs.

There have been many Atlas robots since the project began in 2013. They all share a name and a shape, but the engineering inside has been rebuilt twice.

## Atlas Mark 1 — DARPA, hydraulics, and the famous YouTube videos

The original Atlas was built in 2013 with funding from DARPA for the [Robotics](/atlas/concept/robotics) Challenge — a competition to make robots that could enter dangerous places (think Fukushima) and operate human tools. It stood about 1.88 m tall, weighed 150 kg, and was powered by **hydraulics**: pressurised oil pumped through valves to drive the joints.

Hydraulics are powerful — you can lift heavy weights with them, and they respond fast. They're also messy: they leak, they're heavy, they need a noisy pump, and they're impossible to make quiet enough for a workplace.

That said, this Atlas was the star of robotics for a decade. The viral videos — Atlas jumping onto stacked boxes, backflipping, doing parkour, picking up a toolbox and tossing it — these were the Atlas Mark 1 in action.

## Atlas Mark 2 — Electric, in 2024

In April 2024, Boston [Dynamics](/atlas/concept/dynamics) released a short YouTube video titled "Farewell to HD Atlas." It showed the hydraulic Atlas slowly, dramatically saying goodbye, with falls and stumbles played for emotion. The next day they announced a new Atlas — fully electric, with no hydraulics at all. It looked like a different robot entirely: smoother, more compact, with rotating joints that can spin in ways human bodies can't.

The electric Atlas is the one Hyundai will use in factories. It's quieter, cleaner, lighter, and — crucially — manufacturable at scale. Hydraulic systems were never going to make it into a factory floor next to humans.

## What makes Atlas different from [Tesla](/atlas/company/tesla) [Optimus](/atlas/robot/optimus)

Both are humanoids. Both walk. Both have hands. But the approaches are different.

**Atlas** is the heir to 30 years of dynamic-balance research. It excels at moving through unstructured environments — climbing, jumping, recovering from being pushed. Its software stack uses **whole-body model-predictive control**: at every moment, it's predicting where its body will be a half-second from now, and adjusting accordingly. Very expensive computation, very responsive movement.

**Optimus** is the heir to Tesla's deep-learning-for-driving work. It uses **neural networks trained on lots of demonstration data** to figure out how to do tasks. It's catching up on the locomotion side but is ahead on the "learn new tasks from video" side.

Long-term, the field will probably converge. Atlas will get better at learning new tasks. Optimus will get better at recovering from a shove. The bigger question is which approach scales faster to actual usefulness in factories and homes.

## What it actually does

As of 2026, Atlas is in early commercial pilots — primarily in Hyundai's automotive plants. It moves parts between stations, performs simple assembly steps, and works on tasks that are dangerous or repetitive for humans. It doesn't do parkour at work; it walks carefully, lifts boxes deliberately, and follows a well-defined script.

The acrobatic Atlas was a *demonstration*. The Atlas at work is a careful, reliable, electric machine.
