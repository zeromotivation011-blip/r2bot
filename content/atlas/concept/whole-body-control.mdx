A humanoid reaching for a high shelf must, at the same instant, keep its balance, keep its feet planted, avoid hitting itself, and actually reach the target. You can't solve those one limb at a time — they interact. **Whole-body control** solves them together.

## The problem

A [humanoid](/atlas/concept/humanoid-robot) has 30+ joints and must satisfy **many objectives and constraints at once**:

- **Tasks** — reach the target, look at something, keep the [center of mass](/atlas/concept/center-of-mass) balanced.
- **Constraints** — don't exceed joint limits, keep planted feet from slipping, respect contact forces, avoid self-collision.

Controlling the arm alone would tip the robot; balancing alone would fail to reach. Whole-body control treats the **entire robot as one system** and finds joint commands that serve all goals simultaneously.

## How it works

Whole-body controllers typically pose each control cycle as a **prioritized optimization** (often a quadratic program) over the robot's [equations of motion](/atlas/concept/equations-of-motion). High-priority objectives (don't fall, honor contacts) are satisfied first; lower-priority ones (reach, gaze) are met in the remaining freedom — exploiting the robot's [redundancy](/atlas/concept/inverse-kinematics), the extra degrees of freedom beyond what any single task needs. It generalizes [inverse kinematics](/atlas/concept/inverse-kinematics) from "place one hand" to "satisfy everything at once, including dynamics and forces."

## Where you'll see it

The balance and manipulation controllers on humanoids (Atlas, Digit, Optimus) and on advanced [quadrupeds](/atlas/concept/quadruped-locomotion) that must locomote *and* manipulate. It's what lets these robots do multiple things fluidly — walk while carrying, catch themselves ([push recovery](/atlas/concept/push-recovery)) while reaching, step onto uneven ground while staying upright.

## Why it matters

Whole-body control is the framework that makes truly capable legged, manipulating robots possible. It's the answer to the defining challenge of high-degree-of-freedom robots: doing many interdependent things at once, safely and in real time.
