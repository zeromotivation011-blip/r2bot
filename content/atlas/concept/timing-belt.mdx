To move a motor's power a short distance — to another shaft, or along a rail — you don't always need gears. Often the neat answer is a **timing belt**: a toothed belt that drives pulleys precisely, without slipping.

## What it is

A timing belt (or **synchronous belt**) is a flexible belt with **teeth** on its inner face that **mesh with grooved pulleys**. Because the teeth interlock, the belt **can't slip** — the driven pulley stays in exact positional synchronization with the driver (hence "timing"). This distinguishes it from plain flat or V-belts, which rely on friction and can slip. Spin one pulley and the belt faithfully turns the other, transmitting motion (and a [gear-ratio](/atlas/concept/gear-ratio)-like change if the pulleys differ in size).

## Why robots use it

- **Light and low-inertia.** A belt is far lighter than a gear train, so belt-driven joints and axes move fast with little added mass — good for speed and safety.
- **Quiet and smooth.** No metal-on-metal gear whine.
- **Cheap and simple.** Inexpensive, easy to route power across a distance or around a structure.
- **Some compliance.** The belt's slight stretch cushions shocks a bit (though it also limits stiffness).
- **Remote motors.** Like [cable drives](/atlas/concept/cable-driven-mechanism), belts let the motor sit away from the moving part, reducing limb mass — common on robot arms (belt-driven joints) and gantries.

The classic example: a **3D printer** moves its print head and bed with timing belts — precise, fast, and cheap.

## The limits

- **Stretch and compliance.** Belts stretch under load and aren't perfectly stiff, so they're less precise than a rigid gear train for high-force, high-accuracy joints (though tensioners and stiff belts help).
- **Tension matters.** Too loose and teeth can skip (losing position — a form of [backlash](/atlas/concept/backlash)); too tight and bearings wear. Proper tensioning is key.
- **Wear.** Belts age, crack, and eventually need replacing.
- **Limited torque** versus gears for the same size.

## Why it matters

The timing belt is a workhorse power-transmission element in robotics — a light, quiet, affordable way to move motion where you need it without slippage. It's especially valuable for keeping moving parts light and motors remote, which is why belt drives appear throughout printers, gantries, [servo](/atlas/concept/servo-motor) axes, and robot arms.
