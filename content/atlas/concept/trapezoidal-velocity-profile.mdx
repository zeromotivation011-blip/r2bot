When a robot joint needs to move from one position to another, it can't just snap there — it has to plan a *speed over time*. The simplest good plan is the **trapezoidal velocity profile**.

## The shape

Plot the joint's speed against time and it forms a trapezoid, in three phases:

1. **Accelerate** at a constant rate up to a cruising speed.
2. **Cruise** at that constant speed.
3. **Decelerate** at a constant rate down to a stop, arriving exactly at the target.

## Why it's used everywhere

It hits a sweet spot: **fast but smooth**. Compared to naively commanding a target and letting a [PID](/atlas/concept/pid-controller) loop yank the joint there, a trapezoidal profile respects the motor's **acceleration and velocity limits** — so it doesn't demand impossible [torque](/atlas/concept/torque), doesn't overshoot, and doesn't slam the mechanism. It's the default in CNC machines, [servo](/atlas/concept/servo-motor) drives, 3D printers, and pick-and-place robots. For very short moves there's no room to reach cruise speed, so it becomes a **triangular** profile (accelerate then immediately decelerate).

## The limitation, and the fix

A trapezoidal profile has a hidden roughness: acceleration switches on and off **instantly** at the corners. That instant change is infinite [jerk](/atlas/concept/jerk) — and it shows up as a mechanical "clunk," vibration, and wear, and it can excite a flexible arm into oscillation. Where smoothness matters (delicate payloads, high precision, camera moves), engineers round those corners with an **S-curve** profile, which ramps the acceleration itself so the jerk stays finite.

## Why it matters

The trapezoidal velocity profile is the workhorse of point-to-point motion — the first tool for moving a joint quickly and controllably. Understanding it (and why its sharp corners sometimes aren't good enough) is the entry point to trajectory generation.
