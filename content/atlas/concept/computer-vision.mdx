Computer vision is how a [robot](/atlas/concept/robot) makes sense of what its [camera](/atlas/concept/camera-vision) sees. It turns a flat grid of pixels into "there's a person 3 metres ahead, facing me, walking forward at 1 m/s." It's done a lot more of the heavy lifting in modern [robotics](/atlas/concept/robotics) than you'd guess.

## The five jobs computer vision does for robots

**Classification.** "What is this thing?" — given a picture, output a label: dog, traffic light, screwdriver.

**Detection.** "Where is the thing?" — given a picture, output a bounding box around every object of interest plus its label.

**Segmentation.** "Which pixels are the thing?" — given a picture, output a precise outline of every object, pixel-perfect.

**Depth estimation.** "How far is the thing?" — given a picture (or two from stereo cameras), output a depth map.

**Tracking.** "Where is the same thing going?" — given a sequence of pictures, follow each object frame by frame.

Most robot vision pipelines chain these together: detect objects → estimate their depth → track their motion → decide what to do.

## How it actually works today

Until ~2012, computer vision was hand-engineered: humans wrote rules ("if the pixels here are dark and edge-aligned, it's probably a road"). It mostly didn't work.

After 2012 (the AlexNet moment), the field switched to **convolutional neural networks** (CNNs). You feed millions of labelled pictures to a network. The network learns the features on its own.

After 2021, **transformer-based vision models** (ViT, DINO, SAM) became state of the art. These can be trained on much larger, less-labelled datasets, and they generalise better.

A modern robot like [Optimus](/atlas/robot/optimus) or Figure 03 has a single [neural network](/atlas/concept/neural-network) that does detection, segmentation, depth, and tracking in one pass — at 30 frames per second, running on a few-hundred-watt onboard computer.

## [Tesla](/atlas/company/tesla) vs. Waymo — the classic vision debate

Tesla uses **vision-only** self-driving: just cameras, no [lidar](/atlas/concept/lidar), no radar.

Waymo uses **[sensor fusion](/atlas/concept/sensor-fusion)**: cameras plus lidar plus radar.

Tesla's argument: humans drive with just two cameras (eyes), so cars should be able to. Waymo's argument: cars don't need to be limited to human senses — give them lidar too and they're safer.

Neither approach is finished. The argument is one of the biggest open questions in modern robotics.

## The models an engineer actually reaches for

- **Detection:** the **YOLO** family (v8/v11) — one forward pass, real-time on an onboard GPU — is the default for robots that must react at 30–60 fps. Two-stage detectors (Faster R-CNN) are more accurate but slower.
- **Segmentation:** **Mask R-CNN** for instance masks; **SAM / SAM 2** (Segment Anything) for promptable, zero-shot masks that generalize to objects never seen in training — a big deal for open-world manipulation.
- **Backbones:** CNNs (ResNet, EfficientNet) still win on tiny edge budgets; **Vision Transformers** (ViT, DINOv2) win when you have data and compute, and their features transfer better to downstream robot tasks.
- **Depth:** stereo matching, or monocular depth models (Depth Anything) when you only have one camera.

## How vision quality is measured

You can't tune what you can't score. The core metric is **IoU** (Intersection over Union) — overlap between predicted and true boxes/masks. Detection is summarized by **mAP** (mean Average Precision) across IoU thresholds, the number every benchmark (COCO) reports. For a robot, though, the metric that matters is often downstream: did the grasp succeed? A model with high mAP can still fail the robot if it's wrong on the specific objects and lighting the robot faces.

## Why robot vision is harder than benchmark vision

A model that scores well on internet photos can fall apart on a robot because of the **domain gap**: motion blur, odd viewpoints, harsh or changing lighting, transparent/reflective objects (glass, metal), and cluttered scenes with heavy occlusion. Two consequences dominate real deployments — models trained in **simulation** need domain randomization to survive the **sim-to-real** jump, and latency is a hard constraint: a perfect detection that arrives 200 ms late is useless to a moving robot. This is why perception is co-designed with control, not bolted on.

## Check your understanding

**1.** What's the difference between object *detection* and *segmentation*? Detection outputs a **bounding box + label** per object; segmentation outputs a **per-pixel mask** — a precise outline. Segmentation is heavier but essential for grasping oddly-shaped objects.

**2.** A detector scores high mAP on COCO but the robot keeps mis-grasping — what's likely wrong? The **domain gap**: the robot's lighting, viewpoints, motion blur, or object set differ from the benchmark data. Benchmark accuracy doesn't guarantee task success.

**3.** Why might you pick YOLO over Faster R-CNN on a mobile robot? YOLO is **single-stage and real-time**; on a latency-constrained robot a slightly-less-accurate detection at 60 fps beats a more accurate one that arrives too late to act on.
