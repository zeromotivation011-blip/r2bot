How does a robot *feel* — sense how hard it's gripping, or how heavy an object is? At the bottom of almost every force sensor sits a humble, brilliant device: the **strain gauge**.

## How it works

A strain gauge is a fine metal-foil pattern bonded to a surface. When that surface deforms even microscopically under load, the foil stretches or compresses with it — and stretching a conductor **changes its electrical resistance**. Measure that tiny resistance change and you know the strain, which (through the material's known stiffness) tells you the **force**.

```diagram

```

The resistance change is minuscule, so gauges are wired into a **Wheatstone bridge** — a clever circuit that turns a fractional resistance change into a clean voltage and cancels out temperature drift.

## Where it becomes a robot sense

A bare strain gauge is just the element; it becomes useful when bonded to a purpose-built structure:

- **[Load cells](/atlas/concept/load-cell)** — a metal body with gauges that measures weight or a single force axis (robot scales, payload sensing).
- **[Force-torque sensors](/atlas/concept/force-torque-sensor)** — arrangements of gauges on a machined element that read force *and* torque in all six axes, mounted at a robot's wrist for assembly and [admittance control](/atlas/concept/admittance-control).
- **[Tactile sensors](/atlas/concept/tactile-sensor)** and grippers — sensing contact and grip strength.

## Strengths and quirks

Strain gauges are cheap, small, accurate, and fast. Their main enemies are **temperature** (handled by the bridge and compensation gauges) and **fatigue** — bonded foil under endless flexing eventually cracks, so mounting and material choice matter.

## Why it matters

The strain gauge is the foundational transducer of force in robotics. Every time a robot weighs a payload, regulates grip, or detects contact, a strain gauge is almost certainly doing the sensing underneath.
