[NeRF](/atlas/concept/neural-radiance-field) proved you could reconstruct photorealistic 3D from photos, but it was slow. **3D Gaussian splatting** (2023) delivered nearly the same quality in **real time** — and quickly became the practical successor.

## The idea

Instead of a neural network you query point by point, Gaussian splatting represents the scene **explicitly** as **millions of tiny 3D "Gaussians"** — little fuzzy, colored, semi-transparent blobs, each with a position, size, orientation, color, and opacity. Together they add up to the full scene. To render a view, the blobs are **projected (splatted) onto the image and blended** — a fast rasterization operation, not slow ray marching.

## Why it took over so fast

- **Real-time rendering.** Fast enough to fly through a reconstructed scene interactively — a decisive practical advantage over NeRF's slow rendering.
- **High quality.** Photorealism comparable to (often exceeding) NeRF, with sharp detail.
- **Explicit and editable.** Because the scene is actual 3D primitives (not a black-box network), it's easier to edit, manipulate, and integrate with existing graphics pipelines — and it connects naturally to [point cloud](/atlas/concept/point-cloud) thinking.

## Why it matters for robots

- **Real-time 3D maps and digital twins.** Fast, photorealistic reconstructions of real environments for planning, inspection, and visualization.
- **Simulation and [synthetic data](/atlas/concept/synthetic-data).** Rendering realistic novel views of real places quickly enough to be useful for training and testing.
- **SLAM integration.** A wave of research fuses Gaussian splatting with [SLAM](/atlas/concept/slam) for dense, photorealistic online mapping, and with [semantic mapping](/atlas/concept/semantic-mapping) for meaning-rich reconstructions.

## Why it matters

3D Gaussian splatting made high-fidelity 3D reconstruction **fast enough to be practical**, turning a research marvel into a usable tool. For robotics, it's a leading approach for building rich, real-time, photorealistic 3D representations of the world — a fast-moving frontier in perception and mapping.
